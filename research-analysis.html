Critical Evaluation

Research Analysis

Examining the scientific evidence, the real limitations, and where clinical translation stands today.

The Central Challenge: Biomarker Abundance

The most fundamental limitation of liquid biopsy for early cancer detection is mathematical and biological: cancer biomarkers are present in extraordinarily low abundance in the blood of people with early-stage disease.

A small, early-stage tumor might shed ctDNA at concentrations of 1–10 mutant alleles per milliliter of plasma, while a healthy person’s bloodstream contains tens of millions of normal cell-free DNA fragments. This means detecting a cancer signal requires identifying one mutant fragment among billions of normal ones.

The Numbers

In advanced cancer: ctDNA may constitute 1–10% of total cfDNA (detectable).
In Stage I cancer: ctDNA is often <0.1% of total cfDNA (at the edge of detection limits).
In asymptomatic individuals: tumor-derived DNA fraction may be <0.01% (largely undetectable by current methods).

Sensitivity vs. Specificity: The Core Trade-Off

Diagnostic Accuracy Terminology
  • Sensitivity: Proportion of people with cancer who test positive (true positive rate)
  • Specificity: Proportion of people without cancer who test negative (true negative rate)
  • PPV: Probability a positive result reflects true cancer given disease prevalence
  • NPV: Probability a negative result reflects true absence of cancer

In the DETECT-A trial (Lennon et al., Science 2020), screening 10,006 women without prior cancer history yielded 26 confirmed cancer positives. Of these, 9 had no cancer found after extensive follow-up workup (a positive predictive value (PPV) of ~65%). In a population where cancer prevalence is <1%, even a 0.5% false positive rate would mean 5,000 unnecessary follow-up procedures per million screened. This is why specificity, not just sensitivity, is the critical metric for population screening.

Sources of False Positives

Clonal Hematopoiesis (CHIP)

Age-related somatic mutations in blood stem cells can perfectly mimic cancer driver mutations without ever becoming malignant. CHIP prevalence rises sharply after age 60, making it a major confounder for ctDNA-based tests.

Benign Inflammatory Conditions

Inflammation, infection, tissue remodeling, and certain benign tumors can release DNA fragments that resemble cancer-derived material, triggering false positives.

Technical Artifacts

PCR errors, oxidative DNA damage during sample handling, and sequencing misincorporations can create artificial mutation signals. Error-corrected sequencing (e.g., duplex sequencing) reduces but does not eliminate this.

Normal cfDNA Background

Even healthy individuals have low-level somatic mutations in cfDNA from normal cell turnover. Distinguishing these from truly malignant signals requires knowledge of the patient’s germline and tumor mutation profile.

Sample Handling Variability

cfDNA is fragile and degrades within hours if blood is not processed properly. Hemolysis releases genomic DNA from red cells, massively diluting the ctDNA fraction. Standardization across labs remains poor.

Assay Design Choices

Different tests target different mutation panels, methylation sites, or protein markers. A cancer missed by one assay may be detected by another. No gold-standard assay exists; results are platform-dependent.

False Negatives: Why Tests Miss Early Cancer

  • Low shedding rates: Some tumors, particularly brain cancers and some early carcinomas, shed minimal ctDNA into peripheral blood.
  • Low mutation burden: Microsatellite-stable colorectal cancers and some ovarian cancers have few detectable mutations.
  • Compartmentalization: Tumors inside the blood-brain barrier, peritoneal cavity, or urinary tract shed poorly into peripheral circulation.
  • Technical floor: Current assay sensitivity floors at approximately 0.01% mutant allele fraction; tumor fractions below this level are undetectable.
Critical Point

A negative liquid biopsy does not rule out early-stage cancer. A woman with Stage I breast cancer has approximately a 40–60% chance of a negative liquid biopsy result, depending on the test. This is incompatible with a diagnostic role at present.

Clinical Validation Gaps

Most published liquid biopsy studies have significant methodological limitations:

  • Case-control enrichment bias: Validation cohorts often include 50% cancer cases vs. 50% controls, far higher than real-world prevalence (<1% annually). This artificially inflates PPV estimates.
  • Spectrum bias: Many studies enroll patients with known, symptomatic, or advanced cancers. Results do not translate to asymptomatic screening populations.
  • Short follow-up: Without 5–10 year follow-up, it’s impossible to know if detected signals represent clinically meaningful cancers or indolent lesions that would never harm the patient.
  • Surrogate endpoints: Sensitivity and specificity data are not the same as evidence of mortality reduction, which is the only endpoint that matters for screening programs.
  • Lack of randomized trials: No large randomized controlled trial has yet demonstrated that liquid biopsy screening reduces cancer-specific mortality. The NHS-Galleri trial (NCT02664844) is ongoing.

Where Liquid Biopsy Currently Works

Setting aside early detection screening, liquid biopsy has demonstrated real clinical utility in specific contexts:

Therapy Selection

FDA-approved: cobas EGFR mutation test for NSCLC, FoundationOne Liquid CDx for multiple cancer types. Identifies actionable mutations when tissue is unavailable.

Treatment Monitoring

Serial ctDNA measurements correlate with tumor burden and predict response to chemotherapy or targeted therapy, often weeks ahead of imaging. Clinically useful in breast and lung cancer.

Minimal Residual Disease

Post-surgical ctDNA positivity strongly predicts recurrence in colorectal, lung, and breast cancers. The DYNAMIC trial (2022) demonstrated ctDNA-guided adjuvant chemotherapy is safe and reduces overtreatment.

Resistance Detection

Emergence of resistance mutations (e.g., T790M in EGFR-mutated NSCLC, ESR1 in ER+ breast cancer) detectable in ctDNA before clinical progression, enabling earlier therapy switches.

My Analysis: What the Evidence Suggests

Editorial Interpretation

The literature tells a story of genuine technological innovation constrained by fundamental biology. Liquid biopsy is not a failed technology; it is a technology applied to the wrong problem prematurely. The claims most prominently marketed (catch cancer early from a single blood draw) have outpaced the evidence.

What the research consistently shows: liquid biopsy is excellent at monitoring known cancer and has real FDA-approved utility there. As a population screening tool, it faces three unsolved problems simultaneously: low sensitivity at Stage I, uncertain PPV in true asymptomatic populations, and no randomized trial data on mortality benefit.

The field is progressing rapidly. Methylation-based approaches and multi-analyte tests represent genuine advances over single-biomarker methods. But “promising” is not the same as “ready.” Evidence-based medicine requires more than sensitivity data from enriched cohorts.

Future Directions

  • Randomized screening trials: The NHS-Galleri RCT with 140,000 participants is the most important ongoing trial in the field. Results expected 2026–2027.
  • AI-assisted signal interpretation: Machine learning models applied to multi-analyte data (cfDNA methylation + protein + fragmentomics) may improve specificity by better distinguishing CHIP from true malignancy.
  • Fragmentomics: The size distribution and end-motifs of cfDNA fragments carry tissue-of-origin information independent of mutations, adding a new detection layer.
  • Methylation profiling: Genome-wide methylation patterns are cancer-type specific and detectable at low tumor fractions, currently the most promising technical approach.
  • Equitable access: Current tests cost $500–$10,000 per sample. Widespread screening requires cost reduction and infrastructure investment, particularly for underserved populations.
