Key Studies & Clinical Trials

Landmark research shaping our understanding of liquid biopsy. Each study is summarized with findings, methods, limitations, and why it matters.

Six high-impact studies across early detection, treatment monitoring, and screening feasibility. Every claim on this site is traceable to one of these or the sources listed on the bibliography page.

Early Detection & Multi-Cancer Tests

CancerSEEK: Multi-Analyte Blood Test for Early Cancer Detection

Cohen et al., 2018
Research Question

Can a single blood test combining protein biomarkers and ctDNA mutations detect non-metastatic cancers across eight cancer types?

Study Design

Case-control; 1,005 patients with Stage I–III cancers (8 types) vs. 812 healthy controls. Tested 8 protein biomarkers + 16 ctDNA hotspot mutations.

Main Findings
  • Overall sensitivity: 70% (range 33% [breast] to 98% [ovarian])
  • Specificity: 99% (7 false positives in 812 controls)
  • Correctly identified tissue of origin in 83% of test-positive cases
  • Stage I sensitivity notably lower: ~40% overall
Why It Matters

Demonstrated that a multi-analyte approach combining protein + DNA biomarkers could detect diverse cancers from a single blood draw with clinically relevant specificity. Established proof of concept for multi-cancer early detection (MCED) tests.

Limitations

Case-control design with equal cancer/control representation (not real-world prevalence). Patients had known, diagnosed cancers — not truly asymptomatic. Sensitivity was dramatically lower for Stage I than overall. Does not establish clinical benefit.

Citation

Cohen JD, et al. “Detection and localization of surgically resectable cancers with a multi-analyte blood test.” Science. 2018;359(6378):926–930. PMID: 29348365

Galleri MCED Test: Clinical Validation of Methylation-Based Multi-Cancer Detection

Klein et al., 2021
Research Question

Can genome-wide cfDNA methylation patterns from a blood test accurately detect and localize cancer signal across 50+ cancer types in an independent validation cohort?

Study Design

Independent validation set within CCGA study; 6,689 participants (2,369 cancer, 4,320 non-cancer). Methylation sequencing of cfDNA; ML classifier trained on separate training set.

Main Findings
  • Overall sensitivity: 51.5% at 99.5% specificity
  • Stage I sensitivity: 16.8%; Stage III: 77.4%; Stage IV: 90.1%
  • Tissue of origin correctly predicted in 88.7% of positives
  • 12 cancer types had >75% sensitivity (e.g., anorectal, head/neck, ovarian)
Why It Matters

The most rigorously validated MCED test published. Demonstrated that methylation-based detection achieves high specificity across a truly diverse cancer cohort and can predict tissue of origin at high accuracy — critical for guiding follow-up imaging.

Limitations

Case-control enrichment inflates apparent PPV. Only 16.8% Stage I sensitivity means 5 of 6 early-stage cancers are missed. No evidence yet of mortality reduction in a screened population. NHS-Galleri RCT (N=140,000) is ongoing.

Citation

Klein EA, et al. “Clinical validation of a targeted methylation-based multi-cancer early detection test using an independent validation set.” Ann Oncol. 2021;32(9):1167–1177. PMID: 34044096

DETECT-A Trial: Feasibility of MCED Blood Testing Combined with PET-CT in Asymptomatic Women

Lennon et al., 2020
Research Question

Is CancerSEEK multi-analyte blood testing feasible as a population screening tool in asymptomatic women, and what happens clinically after a positive result?

Study Design

Prospective, single-arm trial. 10,006 women without prior cancer history, ages 65–75. Blood test followed by PET-CT and specialist evaluation for positives.

Main Findings
  • 96 positive blood test results in 10,006 participants (1.0%)
  • PET-CT revealed potential cancers in 36/96 positives
  • 26 cancers confirmed; of these, 19 were not found by standard screening
  • 9 positives had no cancer found despite extensive workup (false positives)
  • PPV of confirmed PET-CT findings: ~72%
Why It Matters

First prospective feasibility study of an MCED test in a true asymptomatic population. Demonstrated detection of cancers not identified by standard screening, but also quantified the false positive burden and illustrated the clinical complexity of follow-up workups.

Limitations

No control arm; cannot determine whether early detection improved outcomes. Only enrolled women. 9 of 96 positives led to extensive, unnecessary workup. No proof of mortality reduction.

Citation

Lennon AM, et al. “Feasibility of blood testing combined with PET-CT to screen for cancer and guide intervention.” Science. 2020;369(6499):eabb9601. PMID: 32630322

Treatment Monitoring & Residual Disease

DYNAMIC Trial: ctDNA-Guided Adjuvant Chemotherapy in Stage II Colon Cancer

Tie et al., 2022
Research Question

Can post-surgical ctDNA status guide adjuvant chemotherapy decisions in Stage II colon cancer, reducing overtreatment without compromising survival outcomes?

Study Design

Randomized controlled trial; 455 patients with Stage II colon cancer post-resection. ctDNA-guided arm: ctDNA+ received chemo, ctDNA− observed. Standard arm: treatment by conventional risk criteria.

Main Findings
  • 15% fewer patients received chemotherapy in ctDNA-guided arm
  • 2-year recurrence-free survival: equivalent between arms (93.5% vs 92.4%)
  • ctDNA+ post-op patients who received chemo had improved outcomes vs. untreated ctDNA+ patients
  • ctDNA− patients with high-risk features had excellent survival without chemo
Why It Matters

The strongest evidence to date that ctDNA-guided treatment decisions in post-surgical cancer management are clinically safe and can reduce unnecessary treatment. This is a landmark in ctDNA’s transition from research tool to clinical practice.

Limitations

Limited to Stage II colon cancer; applicability to other cancer types requires separate validation. Two-year follow-up may not capture all late recurrences. Not powered as a superiority trial.

Citation

Tie J, et al. “Circulating Tumor DNA Analysis Guiding Adjuvant Therapy in Stage II Colon Cancer.” N Engl J Med. 2022;386(24):2261–2272. PMID: 35657951

ctDNA Monitoring in Metastatic Breast Cancer: Comparison to CTCs and CA 15-3

Dawson et al., 2013
Research Question

Does ctDNA level correlate with disease burden and treatment response better than circulating tumor cells (CTCs) or the standard serum protein marker CA 15-3?

Study Design

Prospective cohort; 30 patients with metastatic breast cancer undergoing treatment. Serial blood samples analyzed for ctDNA, CTC count, and CA 15-3. Correlated with imaging and clinical outcomes.

Main Findings
  • ctDNA detectable in 97% of patients; CTCs detected in 87%; CA 15-3 elevated in 78%
  • ctDNA levels correlated most strongly with tumor volume on imaging (r=0.89)
  • ctDNA predicted treatment response and clinical progression earlier than CA 15-3 in most patients
  • In one patient, ctDNA rise preceded CT-confirmed progression by 5 months
Why It Matters

Seminal early study establishing ctDNA as a superior monitoring biomarker over CTCs and protein markers in metastatic breast cancer. Influenced subsequent clinical adoption of ctDNA monitoring and set the standard for serial sampling study design.

Limitations

Small sample (n=30); single-institution study; focused on metastatic disease where ctDNA levels are high. Does not apply to early-stage detection. All patients already had confirmed breast cancer.

Citation

Dawson SJ, et al. “Analysis of circulating tumor DNA to monitor metastatic breast cancer.” N Engl J Med. 2013;368(13):1199–1209. PMID: 23484797

NHS-Galleri Pilot: MCED Test in Asymptomatic NHS Screening Population

Neal et al., 2023
Research Question

What is the real-world performance, positive predictive value, and follow-up burden of the Galleri MCED test in an unselected asymptomatic NHS screening population?

Study Design

Prospective, single-arm pilot; 6,972 participants from 7 NHS Trusts. Participants aged 50–77 with no current cancer diagnosis. Galleri test followed by standard diagnostic workup for positives.

Main Findings
  • 0.5% test-positive rate (35/6,935 valid results)
  • 11 cancers confirmed in 35 positives: PPV = 43.1%
  • Tissue of origin correctly predicted in 75% of confirmed cancers
  • Of 11 confirmed cancers, 5 were Stage I or II at diagnosis
Why It Matters

First published real-world NHS screening pilot with an MCED test. Demonstrates lower PPV in genuine asymptomatic populations (43%) compared to case-control studies (~88%). Provides the most realistic data set for evaluating what population screening would actually look like clinically.

Limitations

Pilot study; single arm; no control group. Follow-up period too short to observe mortality outcome. PPV of 43% means majority of positives are false alarms. The full NHS-Galleri RCT (140,000 participants) will provide definitive evidence.

Citation

Neal RD, et al. “Cell-free DNA-based multi-cancer early detection test in an asymptomatic screening population (NHS-Galleri): a prospective, single-arm, intervention pilot study.” Lancet. 2023;401(10373):264–274. PMID: 36634698
