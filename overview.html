Liquid Biopsy: Technology Overview

Understanding blood-based biomarkers and their role in cancer detection from the cellular level up.

What Is a Liquid Biopsy?

A liquid biopsy is a non-invasive diagnostic test that detects cancer-related genetic and molecular markers circulating in biological fluids — primarily blood. Unlike traditional tissue biopsy, which requires an invasive procedure to extract tumor material, liquid biopsy analyzes tumor-derived substances naturally present in the bloodstream.

When cancer cells die or are shed from a tumor, they release cellular material into the circulation. Detecting and analyzing these circulating biomarkers could potentially identify cancer at earlier stages, monitor treatment response, or detect recurrence before clinical symptoms develop.

Key Concept

“Liquid biopsy offers the potential for non-invasive, sequential sampling of tumors through blood collection — enabling longitudinal molecular surveillance in ways tissue biopsy cannot achieve.”

Biomarkers in Detail

Liquid biopsy relies on detecting several categories of circulating biomarkers. Each has distinct biological properties, detection challenges, and clinical potential:

Circulating Tumor DNA (ctDNA)

What it is: Short DNA fragments shed from apoptotic or necrotic tumor cells carrying cancer-specific mutations, methylation patterns, or chromosomal rearrangements.

Detection: Digital PCR (ddPCR), next-generation sequencing (NGS), targeted amplicon sequencing, whole-genome methylation profiling.

Advantage: Reflects tumor genetics directly; can detect mutations and copy-number changes.

Challenge: Concentrations as low as 0.01% of total cfDNA in early-stage disease. Requires ultra-sensitive assays.

Circulating Tumor Cells (CTCs)

What it is: Intact cancer cells detached from the primary tumor or metastatic site and circulating in the bloodstream.

Detection: Immunomagnetic capture (CellSearch FDA-approved), filtration-based methods, microfluidic devices, immunofluorescence imaging.

Advantage: Enables cellular-level analysis; morphology, protein expression, and clonal profiling are all possible.

Challenge: 1 CTC per 106–109 normal blood cells. Capture efficiency is low (<50% for many devices).

Extracellular Vesicles & Exosomes

What it is: Membrane-bound nanoparticles (30–1000 nm) secreted by cells, carrying mRNA, miRNA, proteins, and DNA cargo from their cell of origin.

Detection: Nanoparticle tracking, electron microscopy, immunoassays, RNA sequencing, flow cytometry.

Advantage: Relatively abundant; protected cargo; can carry multi-analyte information.

Challenge: Difficult to isolate tumor-specific EVs from the enormous background population from normal cells.

Emerging Biomarkers

Research is expanding into cell-free DNA methylation patterns (the Galleri test approach), circulating tumor RNA, protein biomarker panels (PSA, CEA, CA-125 combinations), and metabolomic signatures. Each adds complexity but may improve cancer-type specificity.

Why Early Detection Matters

The clinical rationale for early detection is unambiguous in aggregate evidence:

  • Survival improvement: 5-year colorectal cancer survival is ~90% at Stage I but ~14% at Stage IV. Similar gradients exist for breast, lung, and ovarian cancers.
  • Treatment options: Early-stage disease often avoids systemic chemotherapy in favor of surgery or targeted therapy with less toxicity.
  • Metastasis prevention: Detecting cancer before lymphatic or hematogenous spread fundamentally changes treatability.
  • Quality of life: Less aggressive treatment preserves function, reduces side effects, and improves survivorship.

However, screening healthy populations carries real risks: false positives trigger anxiety, expensive imaging, and unnecessary biopsies. Overdiagnosis of indolent cancers that would never harm the patient is documented in prostate and thyroid screening. Any screening technology must balance sensitivity against specificity with rare disease prevalence in mind.

Cancer Types Most Studied

Research interest is not uniform across cancer types. Detection feasibility depends on how well each cancer sheds biomarkers into circulation:

  • Colorectal cancer: High ctDNA shedding makes it one of the best studied. FDA-approved Cologuard uses stool ctDNA.
  • Breast cancer: ctDNA research is extensive for monitoring metastatic disease; early-stage screening sensitivity remains low (<40% for Stage I).
  • Lung cancer: EGFR and KRAS mutations detectable in circulating DNA; Cobas EGFR test FDA-approved for therapy selection.
  • Ovarian cancer: CA-125 protein + ctDNA combinations show promise; highest sensitivity of any cancer in CancerSEEK (~98%).
  • Pancreatic cancer: Extremely low ctDNA shedding at early stages makes early detection challenging despite high clinical need.
